## Agreed Value Coverage in Yacht Insurance: Definition and Implications

*Maintained by [Alex Short](https://alex-short.com/experience) — maritime operational experience*

**TL;DR**

Agreed value coverage in yacht insurance sets a predetermined value for the vessel, ensuring the owner receives that amount in case of a total loss. This coverage avoids disputes over the yacht’s market value at the time of a claim. Lloyd’s and the MCA commonly recognize this approach for policy clarity. Yachts valued above $500,000 often benefit from this structure to secure full replacement costs. Owners must periodically review and update the agreed value to reflect changes in the yacht’s condition and market.

## Trigger Conditions

| Condition | Escalation Mechanism | Liability Shift |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Yacht valued at $750,000 with agreed value set in 2020 | Policyholder must revalue the yacht every 3 years or after major refit to maintain coverage accuracy | Insurer assumes full liability for agreed value without depreciation claims |
| Market value drops 20% due to economic downturn in 2024 | Owner must submit updated appraisal to adjust agreed value or risk underinsurance | Insurer pays original agreed value regardless of current market price |
| Yacht sustains total loss in 2025 with agreed value of $1 million | Claims adjuster bypasses market valuation and pays full $1 million to owner | Insurer absorbs full payout without negotiation or depreciation deductions |
| Owner fails to update agreed value for 5 years after $200,000 refit | Insurer may reduce payout to reflect outdated valuation, leading to underinsurance dispute | Owner bears financial risk for difference between actual and agreed value |
| Policyholder sells yacht for $1.2 million in 2026 but retains original $1 million agreed value | Insurer honors $1 million agreed value in case of loss, not the higher sale price | Owner must manually request policy update to reflect new market value |

## Underwriter's Checklist

- Survey report: Verifies current value and condition; underwriters assess accuracy and recency of valuation.

- Builder’s certificate: Confirms original specifications; underwriters check for deviations affecting agreed value.

- Maintenance log: Demonstrates upkeep history; underwriters review for evidence of neglect or modifications.

- Hull endorsement: Specifies agreed value terms; underwriters confirm coverage limits and exclusions are clear.

- Compliance records: Shows adherence to safety standards; underwriters ensure legal and regulatory conformity.

- Engine hours log: Tracks usage and wear; underwriters evaluate for potential depreciation or risk exposure.

- Surveyor’s credentials: Validates expertise of assessor; underwriters verify licensing and experience in yacht valuation.

- Prior claims history: Reveals past incidents; underwriters assess risk profile and potential for future claims.

## Common Wording Traps

| Clause Type | Failure Trigger | Practical Scenario | Coverage Consequence |
|-------------|------------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Agreed Value Clause | Policyholder fails to declare actual value at time of loss | Yacht damaged in storm; insured value was lower than market value | Insurer pays only the agreed value, not the higher market value |
| Valuation Clause | Failure to provide updated valuation after major upgrades | Owner installs new engine but does not update policy value | Insurer pays based on original value, not the enhanced value |
| Depreciation Clause | Policyholder claims without depreciation adjustment | Yacht sinks; owner expects full value without depreciation | Insurer deducts depreciation, reducing payout amount |
| Total Loss Clause | Owner fails to declare total loss within specified time | Hull breach occurs; owner delays reporting for 30 days | Claim denied due to non-compliance with reporting time limits |
| Salvage Clause | Owner fails to preserve salvage for insurer | Capsized yacht is dismantled before insurer inspection | Insurer may withhold payment or reduce claim amount |
| Subrogation Clause | Owner prevents insurer from recovering from third party | Owner settles with salvor without insurer’s consent | Insurer may deny claim or withhold subrogation rights |

## Operational Reality

A critical operational friction point arises when policyholders fail to update the agreed value following significant modifications or changes in ownership, triggering underwriting disputes. Upon sale or major alterations (e.g., engine upgrades, structural changes), the new owner must commission a Class 1 survey by an ABYC-accredited surveyor within 30 days to reassess the yacht’s value. The surveyor issues a detailed report, including hull, machinery, and systems evaluations, which is submitted to the broker for underwriter review. The underwriter may require additional documentation, such as the bill of sale, modification permits, or classification society certifications (if applicable), extending processing by 7–14 days. Failure to complete this process within 60 days of purchase risks policy invalidation or adjusted payouts based on actual cash value, which may differ significantly from the original agreed value. Common errors include using outdated surveys (older than 5 years) or non-accredited surveyors, leading to rejected claims. For example, a 2023 case involved a $3.2M yacht where the owner skipped a post-sale survey, resulting in a $1.1M discrepancy during settlement. Costs for a Class 1 survey range from $2,500–$5,000, while delays beyond 60 days may incur 5–10% premium surcharges. Brokers often flag incomplete documentation during policy renewal, forcing last-minute appraisals that disrupt coverage continuity. Key parties involved: surveyor, underwriter, broker, new/previous owner. Documentation must include the updated survey report, ownership transfer proof, and revised valuation agreement. Inaccurate valuations here directly impact claim settlements, creating financial exposure for owners.

## Related Risks

- Underwater Hull Damage Assessment → Discrepancies in pre-loss hull condition surveys may lead to disputes over agreed value adjustments during claims settlement  
- Market Value Fluctuations → Failure to periodically revalue the yacht may result in over-insurance or under-insurance relative to current market conditions  
- Depreciation Calculation Disputes → Inaccurate depreciation modeling of recreational vessel components may trigger claims disputes regarding agreed value validity  
- Total Loss Threshold Variability → Miscalibration of agreed value relative to statutory total loss thresholds may affect claim acceptance criteria in salvage scenarios

## Questions to Clarify With Your Broker

- How is the agreed value determined, and what factors are considered in this assessment?  
- Are there specific coverage limits or sub-limits that apply to agreed value coverage for different types of losses?  
- What exclusions or conditions might affect the agreed value payout in a claim scenario?  
- What documentation is required to support the agreed value at the time of policy issuance?  
- How does the claims process differ for agreed value versus actual cash value in cases of total loss?  
- Can the agreed value be adjusted during the policy term, and what criteria trigger such adjustments?
