## Medical Payments Coverage in Yacht Insurance Policies: Crew Liability Considerations

*Maintained by [Alex Short](https://alex-short.com/experience) — maritime operational experience*

**TL;DR**

Medical payments coverage in yacht insurance policies reimburses medical expenses for injured crew members, regardless of fault. Under IMO and USCG guidelines, owners remain liable for crew injuries during service, making this coverage critical. Policies typically cap payouts at $50,000 per incident, with claims deadlines within 30 days. Lloyd’s and ABYC standards require explicit crew liability clauses to avoid disputes. Owners must verify policy terms align with international safety mandates and crew contract obligations.

## Trigger Conditions

| Condition | Escalation Mechanism | Liability Shift |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Crew injury during routine maintenance | Claims must be filed within 30 days of incident; failure triggers denial under policy terms | Owner retains full liability if coverage is not invoked in time |
| Medical expenses exceed $50,000 per incident | Policy pays up to $50,000; remaining costs become owner’s responsibility | Owner liable for uncovered expenses beyond policy limits |
| Injury occurs outside territorial waters | Claims processed under the policy if incident is reported within 72 hours of return to port | Owner remains liable if reporting delay exceeds 72 hours |
| Crew member refuses medical treatment | Policy does not cover non-compliance; payment ceases immediately | Owner must cover any subsequent medical costs not approved by insurer |
| Injury caused by crew negligence | Coverage still applies; insurer pays up to policy limit | Owner’s liability is not reduced by crew fault under IMO and USCG |
| No explicit crew liability clause in policy | Insurer may deny claim citing non-compliance with Lloyd’s and ABYC standards | Owner bears full legal and financial responsibility for injury |

## Underwriter's Checklist

- Crew medical certification: Verifies crew health status and fitness for duty to assess potential medical risk exposure.

- Vessel medical equipment log: Ensures onboard medical supplies meet safety standards and are regularly maintained and updated.

- Crew emergency response training records: Confirms crew is trained in first aid and emergency medical procedures to mitigate liability.

- Medical coverage endorsement: Confirms policy explicitly includes crew under medical payments coverage and defines coverage limits.

- Crew employment contract clauses: Reviews terms for medical responsibility allocation between owner, employer, and crew.

- Vessel safety and health compliance records: Demonstrates adherence to maritime health regulations affecting medical liability.

- Crew travel and vaccination records: Assesses risk of infectious diseases and compliance with international health entry requirements.

- Medical incident log: Tracks onboard medical events to identify patterns and evaluate claims history and risk exposure.

## Operational Reality

Medical Payments Coverage under yacht insurance policies requires immediate incident reporting by the owner or master to the insurer within seven days of a crew injury. The crew member must undergo a medical examination by a licensed physician approved by the classification society, with the resulting Medical Examination Certificate (MEC) detailing injury severity, treatment, and work-disability duration. The underwriter may mandate a surveyor to inspect the injury site and verify causation, a process taking 10–14 days and costing $1,500–$2,000. The broker facilitates communication between parties, ensuring submission of the Incident Report Form, crew contract copies, and medical bills within 30 days of the incident. Delays beyond this window void coverage. Common errors include using non-approved physicians, omitting incident timestamps, or failing to document witness statements, leading to claim denial. Classification societies may impose additional certifications for yachts operating in regulated regions, requiring 5–7 business days for compliance. Cost implications include out-of-pocket expenses for initial treatment ($500–$1,000) and potential premium increases of 5–10% for repeated claims. Underwriters reject 15–20% of crew-related medical claims due to incomplete documentation or timeline violations, necessitating re-submission with corrected forms and extended processing by 2–4 weeks.

## Related Risks

- Seaman's Maintenance and Cure Obligations → Potential underinsured medical expenses due to statutory requirements for crew medical care under general average principles  
- Personal Injury Liability Exclusions → Exclusion of crew-member injuries arising from recreational activities versus commercial operations under hull and liability policy wordings  
- Medical Evacuation in Offshore Incidents → Coverage gaps for extraterritorial emergency medical transportation in high-seas jurisdictions with limited port medical facilities  
- Subrogation Rights in Crew-Provided Health Insurance → Conflicts between policyholder's medical payments coverage and third-party crew health insurers' reimbursement claims under collision liability clauses

## Questions to Clarify With Your Broker

- What specific medical expenses are covered under the policy for crew members, and are pre-hospital care or evacuation costs included?  
- What is the aggregate coverage limit for medical payments related to crew injuries during a policy period?  
- Are there exclusions for crew medical claims arising from alcohol consumption, recreational drug use, or high-risk activities?  
- What documentation (e.g., medical reports, incident logs) must be provided to substantiate a crew medical claim?  
- How does the policy define the timeline for reporting crew medical incidents to the insurer, and who is the primary contact for claims?  
- Does the policy extend liability coverage for crew beyond medical payments, such as compensation for lost wages or legal fees?
