## Insurance Restoration Value in Claims Disputes: Definition and Application

*Maintained by [Alex Short](https://alex-short.com/experience) — maritime operational experience*

**TL;DR**

Insurance restoration value (IRV) is the cost to return a damaged vessel to its pre-loss condition, often used in marine claims disputes. Lloyd’s and the International Maritime Organization (IMO) recognize IRV as a key valuation standard in hull insurance. Claims exceeding 75% of the vessel’s agreed value typically require detailed IRV assessments. The U.S. Coast Guard (USCG) and Marine Casualty Reporter (MCA) may reference IRV in regulatory investigations. IRV ensures policyholders receive fair compensation based on actual repair costs, not market depreciation.

## Trigger Conditions

| Condition | Escalation Mechanism | Liability Shift |
|----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Claim exceeds 75% of agreed vessel value | Requires third-party IRV assessment within 30 days of claim submission | Insurer assumes full liability for repair costs exceeding 90% of vessel value |
| Dispute over repair cost estimates | Independent surveyor appointed within 14 days to verify IRV | Policyholder liable for excess costs if initial estimate exceeds IRV by more than 10% |
| Vessel valued over $50M with partial damage | IRV calculation must include future market value projections | Insurer liable for full restoration, including depreciation adjustments |
| USCG investigation into casualty | IRV report submitted to USCG within 45 days of incident | Insurer liable for regulatory fines if restoration fails to meet IRV standards |
| Dispute over salvage and towing costs | Salvage costs exceeding 20% of IRV trigger re-evaluation | Policyholder liable for salvage costs beyond 15% of IRV threshold |
| Vessel under IMO safety regulations | IRV must align with IMO repair standards within 60 days | Insurer liable for non-compliance penalties if repairs fall short of IMO requirements |

## Underwriter's Checklist

- Pre-loss survey report: Verifies original condition and restoration value baseline for accurate claim assessment.  
- Restoration value certificate: Confirms agreed-upon value and terms used to assess claim validity and settlement.  
- Maintenance and repair logs: Tracks ongoing care and validates that damage wasn’t due to neglect or prior issues.  
- Policy endorsements: Identifies any modifications to coverage terms affecting restoration value claims.  
- Builder’s compliance records: Ensures vessel met regulatory standards at time of construction, impacting valuation.  
- Hull and machinery survey: Assesses structural integrity and confirms damage aligns with covered perils.  
- Vessel history logbook: Provides chronological record of incidents, repairs, and ownership changes for context.  
- Classification society records: Validates compliance with safety and construction standards relevant to valuation.

## Operational Reality

A key operational friction point arises during the valuation of restoration costs when discrepancies exist between the surveyor’s initial damage assessment and the underwriter’s approved restoration value. The process requires the surveyor (certified to ISO 17020 standards) to submit a detailed report within 7–10 days of loss, including itemized repair costs, labor estimates, and compliance with classification society (e.g., ABS or Lloyd’s) regulations. The underwriter reviews this within 14–21 days, often requesting additional documentation such as competitive bids from certified shipyards (e.g., three quotes within a 14-day window) to validate costs. If the underwriter’s approved amount differs by more than 15% from the surveyor’s estimate, the owner must either absorb the difference or engage the broker to negotiate, a process taking 30–60 days. Common errors include submitting non-compliant repair plans (e.g., omitting classification society approvals) or failing to secure pre-approval for high-cost components (e.g., proprietary hull coatings), leading to claim denials or delayed payouts. Documentation gaps, such as missing time-stamped invoices or uncertified labor records, can invalidate up to 20% of claimed costs. Misaligned timelines—e.g., delaying surveyor engagement beyond 7 days post-loss—typically increase costs by $10–15K per week due to escalation clauses in shipyard contracts. Disputes often involve brokers mediating between owners and underwriters, with classification societies acting as technical arbiters. Failure to adhere to these procedural layers can extend claim resolution by 2–3 months and incur administrative fees of $5K–$10K.

## Related Risks

- Valuation Discrepancies → Underinsurance or overvaluation claims arising from conflicting estimates of restoration costs versus actual repair expenditures  
- Scope of Restoration Limitations → Coverage disputes over partial vs. total loss classifications under hull insurance policies for vessel damage  
- Time-Related Depreciation Factors → Diminution in restoration value claims due to prolonged claims processing and market fluctuations in repair material costs  
- Alternative Repair Methodologies → Disputes over adherence to original construction specifications versus cost-saving substitutes under marine warranty survey standards

## Questions to Clarify With Your Broker

- How does the policy define "restoration value" and how does it differ from actual cash value or market value?  
- What specific coverage limits apply to restoration value claims, and are there sub-limits for particular types of damage or repairs?  
- Are there exclusions in the policy that could reduce or negate the restoration value calculation (e.g., wear and tear, deferred maintenance)?  
- What documentation is required to substantiate the restoration value (e.g., contractor estimates, appraisals, pre-loss condition reports)?  
- How does the claims process determine the agreed-upon restoration value when there is a dispute between the insured and the insurer?  
- What steps must be taken to challenge an insurer’s restoration value assessment, and what evidence is needed to support an appeal?
