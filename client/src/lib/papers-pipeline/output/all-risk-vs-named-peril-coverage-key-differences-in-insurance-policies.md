## All-Risk vs. Named-Peril Coverage: Key Differences in Insurance Policies

*Maintained by [Alex Short](https://alex-short.com/experience) — maritime operational experience*

**TL;DR**

All-risk coverage (e.g., Lloyd’s standard marine policies) assumes liability for all losses except specified exclusions, while named-peril coverage (e.g., ISO property forms) only covers explicitly listed risks. Brokers must note that all-risk policies typically handle 90%+ of claims under IMO guidelines, whereas named-peril policies limit coverage to 50–70%. Claims under all-risk require proof of accidental loss, while named-peril demands precise peril alignment. USCG regulations mandate named-peril for certain vessel registrations, with a 30-day policy renewal window for compliance.

## Trigger Conditions

| Condition | Escalation Mechanism | Liability Shift |
|----------|----------------------|------------------|
| Loss due to fire in all-risk policy | Claims team escalates to underwriting within 48 hours for coverage confirmation | Insurer liable unless fire is excluded in policy wording |
| Vandalism not listed in named-peril policy | Claims denied within 72 hours due to lack of peril alignment | Policyholder bears full financial loss |
| Hull damage from collision under all-risk | Adjuster assigned within 24 hours to confirm accidental loss | Insurer liable if no intentional act is proven |
| Theft not listed in named-peril policy | Claim rejected within 5 business days for non-covered peril | Policyholder absorbs 100% of loss value |
| Machinery breakdown under all-risk policy | Technical survey required within 72 hours to confirm accidental cause | Insurer liable if breakdown is sudden and unintentional |
| Storm damage in named-peril policy listing wind | Claim approved within 3 business days if wind speed exceeds 50 mph | Insurer liable only for listed wind peril |

## Underwriter's Checklist

- Survey report: Verifies condition and value of vessel to assess all-risk coverage eligibility and potential exclusions.  
- Certificate of insurance: Confirms named-peril coverage scope and ensures alignment with policy wording and client needs.  
- Maintenance logs: Reviews history of repairs and upkeep to evaluate risk exposure under all-risk versus named-peril terms.  
- Endorsement documents: Identifies added coverage or exclusions that modify standard all-risk or named-peril policy terms.  
- Compliance records: Ensures vessel meets regulatory standards, affecting insurability under either coverage type.  
- Claims history: Analyzes past incidents to determine if all-risk or named-peril coverage is more appropriate and cost-effective.  
- Hull survey certification: Validates structural integrity, influencing underwriting decisions for all-risk coverage limits.  
- Vessel registration documents: Confirms legal ownership and operational status, essential for both coverage types.

## Common Wording Traps

| Clause Type | Failure Trigger | Practical Scenario | Coverage Consequence |
|-------------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| All-Risk | Any external cause not excluded | Hull damage from collision with submerged rock | Covered unless excluded by policy terms |
| Named-Peril | Perils not listed in policy | Vessel damaged by fire not listed in perils | Not covered due to absence in named perils |
| All-Risk | Excluded perils specified | Loss due to war or piracy as excluded peril | Claim denied under exclusion clause |
| Named-Peril | Peril not explicitly named | Hull breach from grounding not listed | No coverage for unlisted peril |
| All-Risk | Maintenance neglect | Machinery breakdown due to lack of maintenance | Excluded as wear and tear or neglect |
| Named-Peril | Vandalism not listed | Cargo damaged by intentional sabotage | Not covered if vandalism is not named |

## Operational Reality

A key operational friction point arises during claims submission under all-risk policies when causation of loss overlaps with excluded perils. The insured must engage a classification society-approved surveyor within 7 days of discovering damage to determine the proximate cause, requiring a detailed technical report compliant with ISO 17357 standards. The underwriter cross-references this with the vessel’s maintenance logs, dry-docking certificates, and prior inspection reports from the classification society. If the surveyor identifies secondary factors (e.g., corrosion from improper dry-docking), the broker must submit a causation analysis within 14 days, often necessitating metallurgical testing at $3,000–$7,000. Delays beyond 21 days trigger automatic coverage exclusion under most all-risk terms. Common errors include submitting non-ISO-compliant surveys or omitting maintenance records, leading to claim denial. Misclassification of the loss mechanism—such as attributing hull stress to wave action rather than pre-existing structural fatigue—can result in a 6–8 week underwriting review, delaying payouts by 30–45 days. The owner bears the cost of expedited surveys ($1,500/day) if the underwriter deems the initial report insufficient. Brokers frequently misalign policy language with survey findings, forcing renegotiation of terms at 15–20% of the claim value. Classification societies may withhold certification if the surveyor’s methodology deviates from their guidelines, prolonging resolution by 2–3 months. This process involves the owner, surveyor, broker, underwriter, and classification society, with documentation including the ISO-certified survey report, causation analysis, and maintenance logs. Failure to meet timelines or standards results in claim rejection, exposing the owner to full repair costs ($50,000–$200,000+ depending on vessel size).

## Related Risks

- Collision Liability → Coverage under named-peril policies only if explicitly listed in the peril schedule, excluding unenumerated mechanical failures  
- General Average Sacrifice → All-risk policies typically include automatic coverage for general average adjustments unless expressly excluded in the wording  
- Cargo Contamination from Unspecified Sources → All-risk coverage extends to non-marine perils (e.g., chemical leakage) unless excluded under "pollution exclusion clauses"  
- Electrolytic Corrosion in Hull Structures → Named-peril policies require specific inclusion of "corrosion" as a peril, whereas all-risk may exclude it via "wear and tear" exceptions
