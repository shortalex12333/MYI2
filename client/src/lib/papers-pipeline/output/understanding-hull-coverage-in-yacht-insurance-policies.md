## Understanding Hull Coverage in Yacht Insurance Policies

*Maintained by [Alex Short](https://alex-short.com/experience) — maritime operational experience*

Hull coverage in yacht insurance policies protects the physical structure of the vessel against damage or loss from covered perils like collision, fire, or storm. It is typically outlined under frameworks by Lloyd’s and the International Maritime Organization (IMO). The coverage amount should align with the yacht’s current market value, with policies often requiring annual valuation updates. Claims are subject to deductibles, which may range from 5% to 15% of the hull value. This coverage is essential for compliance with U.S. Coast Guard (USCG) and Marine Casualty Investigation Board (MCA) safety and registration standards.

| Hull damage from grounding during low visibility | Incident reported to MCA within 24 hours; navigational logs reviewed within 72 hours | Operator liable if deviation from charted route is confirmed |

## Underwriter's Checklist

- **Recent survey report**: Verifies hull condition, structural integrity, and identifies pre-existing damage affecting coverage scope.  
- **Builder’s original survey certificate**: Confirms compliance with construction standards and hull design specifications at time of build.  
- **Maintenance and repair logs**: Tracks history of hull repairs, corrosion treatment, and maintenance to assess risk of deterioration.  
- **Classification society certificate**: Ensures hull meets recognized safety and structural standards for ongoing compliance.  
- **Hull endorsement details**: Identifies any exclusions, limitations, or additional terms specifically applied to hull coverage.  
- **Anti-fouling system certification**: Validates compliance with environmental regulations and hull protection against marine growth.  
- **Dry dock inspection records**: Documents periodic hull inspections for cracks, leaks, or damage requiring repair.  
- **Compliance with load line regulations**: Confirms hull’s load capacity markings meet international safety and stability requirements.

## Operational Reality

The operational friction point arises during the initial hull survey and certification process, which must be completed within 30 days of policy inception. The owner arranges for a certified surveyor to inspect the hull’s structural integrity, corrosion levels, and compliance with classification society standards (e.g., Lloyd’s Register or ABS). The surveyor generates a detailed report, including photographic evidence, thickness measurements, and a risk assessment, which is submitted to the underwriter for approval. Concurrently, the broker ensures the yacht’s maintenance logs, dry-docking records, and prior survey reports are provided to substantiate claims of ongoing compliance. If the survey identifies deviations from classification society rules—such as unapproved modifications or excessive hull thinning—the underwriter may demand corrective work, extending the timeline by 4–8 weeks and increasing costs by $5,000–$20,000 for repairs. Delays beyond the 30-day window risk policy voidance or retroactive premium adjustments (typically +10–15%). Common errors include incomplete maintenance documentation, which forces rescheduling and additional surveyor fees ($1,500–$3,000), or failure to address prior survey recommendations, leading to claim denials during hull damage disputes. The classification society may also revoke compliance status if defects are not rectified within 90 days of notification, triggering higher insurance rates or non-renewal. All parties must retain the original survey report, classification certificates, and repair invoices for audit purposes, as missing documents invalidate coverage for surveyed defects.

- Electrolytic Corrosion → Hull Material Degradation  
- Storm-Induced Hull Stress → Fatigue Fracture Coverage

## Questions to Clarify With Your Broker

- What specific policy terms define the scope of hull coverage, and how are "total loss" and "partial loss" determined?  
- What are the coverage limits for hull damage, and how is the insured value of the yacht calculated or adjusted over time?  
- Are there exclusions for hull damage caused by poor maintenance, neglect, or specific environmental conditions like freezing temperatures?  
- What documentation is required to substantiate hull damage claims, including survey reports or repair estimates?  
- How does the claims process differ for hull coverage versus other policy components, and what is the typical timeline for settlement?  
- Are there deductible structures specific to hull claims, and how do they apply to multiple incidents within a policy period?
